Smoking Still Kills, Tobacco/Smoking Companies Still Rule

Tobacco consumption is endemic in impoverished and forgotten America. Have you ever realized how many smoke shops you see in more run-down strip malls and within remote gas stations, or did you know even that tobacco advertising is rampant in places without secure housing? More generally, can we use data science to identify and even exploit unconventional indicators of socioeconomic status to bring changes to our communities? Our project Cessation aims to bring more factors into the conversation of poverty in our home city Houston, namely the prevalence of tobacco by area and its potential connection with poverty.

An initial literature review exposed both damning and promising facts about smoking, mortality, and poverty, shown below. The main conclusions to draw are that any relation between smoking and poverty seems to be independent of the percentage of minority folk in a given area, which completely rids the analysis of a racially-charged and biased social indicator. Also, despite major worldwide productivity losses due to tobacco, anti-tobacco campaigns are relatively simple to implement with tangible effects. Based on this review, we again attempt to draw a connection between smoke shops and poverty, even without using common instrumental variables like racial makeup, and propose actionable policy decisions to better our community.

Using Machine Learning to Find What Correlates With Smoke Retailers

Houston smoking vendor location data was obtained from the Texas Open Data Portal. Median income and population data corresponding to Harris County zip codes were also obtained from ZipAtlas. We initially worked to parse, clean, and wrangle our data using the Google Maps API alongside statistical softwares R, Excel, and QGIS. Each data set was then plotted onto a lat-long map and further processed to show heatmaps in income, population density, and smoke shop density.

To find probable causes of median income trends, a least absolute shrinkage and selection operator (or lasso) algorithm was run using location geodata, smoke vendor density, and other variables to determine strong correlations. Lasso regression is a type of linear regression that shrinks data values towards a central point. This shrinking does not have to occur equally across system variables, which results in 'weights' that can give information about which variables correlate to the result the best. We chose the LASSO method, as it is strong in sets with few parameters and this method would give us insight on certain parts of model selection, like variable selection/parameter elimination.

Proposal: Incentives Rather Than Punishments May Help Reduce Smoking Rate

After reviewing the results of our data analysis, we've found evidence that supports a correlation between median income and density of smoke shops in regions in Houston. According to the American Lung Association, one of the primary methods of combatting tobacco usage has been to implement and increase taxes on tobacco sales. In addition, many states have tried implementing an age restriction for purchasing tobacco and tobacco accessories.

Since our results suggest that low median income and harsh socioeconomic situations can make a region more susceptible to tobacco consumption and marketing, we propose that harsher tax and economic penalties will not improve, but exascerbate these specific areas. According to the World Health Organization, tobacco is affected by demand elasticity meaning that when prices increase, consumption decreases by a lesser percentage compared to the price increase. The brunt of these taxes won't be felt by the tobacco vendors, but would instead impact consumers and drive them deeper into economic hardship.

We instead support the approach developed by the American Cancer Society, named the Freshstart program, which attempts to tackle the issue using positive reinforcement rather than through legal punishment. Freshstart provides patients with personal coaching and sessions to guide users towards cesession. We propose that in addition to proliferating this program, that users be offered economic incentives such as tax breaks for succeeding in metrics determined by this program (for example, increased lung health, frequent attendance, etc.). A similar program could be implemented for companies that exclude tobacco products from their stores. Tobacco-only companies would experience a higher tax for opening in low-income areas, so as to decrease predatory marketing.
